A Diversified Global Footprint

Bosch's resilience stems from its diversified structure across four business sectors and a "local for local" strategy that adapts its operations to key markets. This structure is underpinned by a unique ownership model that ensures long-term stability and entrepreneurial freedom.

Four Pillars of Business

The Ownership Advantage

A key to Bosch's strategy is its ownership structure. 94% of the share capital is held by Robert Bosch Stiftung GmbH, a charitable foundation with no voting rights. The majority of voting rights are held by Robert Bosch Industrietreuhand KG, which safeguards the company's long-term existence. This unique setup liberates Bosch from short-term market pressures, enabling significant, long-term investments in R&D and sustainability, which are fundamental to its sustained innovation and strategic resilience.

Future Outlook & Adaptation

Bosch continuously adapts its strategy by learning from the past and investing in the future. Its venture capital arm scouts for disruptive technologies, while strategic pivots ensure the company remains aligned with long-term market trends.

Scouting the Future: Robert Bosch Venture Capital (RBVC)

RBVC is Bosch's corporate venture arm, investing in deep-tech startups to fuel innovation. The "Open Bosch" program connects these startups with Bosch's operating units, creating a powerful ecosystem for co-innovation.

Timeline of Strategic Adaptation

1920s: Diversification

In response to an auto industry crisis, Bosch created a new department (BTH3) to actively seek new business ideas, reducing its dependency on a single sector.

2020: Carbon Neutrality

Achieved carbon neutrality (Scopes 1 & 2) across its 450+ locations, a milestone in its long-term sustainability journey.

2023: Divestment & Focus

Announced plans to sell parts of its Building Technologies division to focus on the higher-growth systems integration business, demonstrating dynamic portfolio management.

2024: Mobility Realignment

Realigned the Mobility sector to create horizontal responsibility for software, semiconductors, and vehicle computers, adapting to the era of the software-defined vehicle.
